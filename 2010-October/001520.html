That sounds very refreshing - but your code speaks differently, as I see
it. For me, it is important to have some special feature's implentation
detailed either by a comment alone, or by a combination of "telltale" names
and comments. For that, I heartily dislike the so-called "Hungarian
Notation" of Borland legacy. It even can confound the reader completely by
disfiguring it to a degree that makes it completely unreadable. In any way,
it makes it unspeakable.
Beyond that, your view is the one of a "super developper", who does all of
a project himself, and only has to keep enough information on hand to know
himself what he did, need to do yet and should do for refinement.
Documentation for _users_ has to a lot more primitive - it has to present
the structural detail you know by heart, rather than explain specific
implementation decisions, usually.
_This_ kind of documentation is best _not _ done by the developper himself.

So that's just what I started out with: The project has grown to a size
where you had to put in more effort than you can afford to keep it public
as it is now.
You probabely have two choices here:
- One, find a couple guys who volunteer for building a documentation system
(there were discussions about that, and there seem to be such people), have
_them_ do the whole work and do just the reviewing. It might be that even
the latter could be to much already for a single person.
- Or, create a _team_ of people to develop your system. Each member would
be resonsible for a specified part of it, including documentation; and this
way, you would have a vital interest to get good documentation, because
you would "degrade" to a regular user, too. I guess this will not at all
appeal to you, though...

The remaining possibility is that your project will keep on stumbling
along, with a group of loyal users that use it for different reasons, but
each one only to a very small degree. The only one capable of using it fully
will still be just you; sort of, at least, because of all the patches added
on user request.

> DEB package's "preinst" & "postinst" allow to make the package a large

A complex, opaque bunch that does unknown and sometimes even disruptive
things to their user's machines. Although the processes often work without
causing noticeable problems, there are cases when something doesn't work
right afterwards, or things don't work any more the packet shouldn't have
touched at all, or even the system won't start again altogether.
Packetized updating does have its problems in any case - I found out yet
again, as I've just updated two machines (Arch Linux, not Debian), where
quite some subtle (and some not-so-subtle) issues arose. It took me quite
some time to readjust most of them, some only surfaced during early use,
when I couldn't address them, and some I just simply haven't seen yet.

If you present these issues to an unknowledgable user, what do you think he
will do and say?

With Windows, the most prominent measure is "reinstall" - loosing most, if
not all of your personal setup. And that's why most Windows users have
already resigned and use all of their software "as is" - forgoing the
purpose of all those delicately engineered settings that could be used to
adjust the workings to their needs, just using them in the crudest way
possible, because the time spent for personalization will be lost in time...
